Instructions

This website asks you, the viewer, to explore an interactive map of New York City. It invites you to imagine maps such as this as tools of urban governance. By governance I mean administration, management, and a general approach to the operation of government (see more on Foucault and Rose for this). Information visualization is a practice central to administration of public data; visualizations render information into representations that can trigger action. It is thus important to consider how and in what ways visualizations create impetus for action (or inaction).

The map you will explore in the next tab ("Visualizations") depicts data from the US Census Bureau's American Community Survey, particularly data on plumbing facilities, using the D3.JS library. Under the Housing Characteristics section of the survey, it reports whether a building has adequate or inadequate plumbing facilities. This is sorted into owner-occupied and renter-occupied, and the available data is organized at the level of Census tract. (ADD MORE ON TRACTS)

Plumbing is an interesting question to look at because it is an aspect of "housing" that is often presumed or taken for granted. An article published in The Guardian in 2021 described the phenomenon of "plumbing poverty" in the United States, where nearly half a million households lack adequate indoor plumbing. Although plumbing poverty has decreased in New York City, it is still significant. It is also a point of intersection between the built environment, housing, government administration, and social services. It can indicate a point at which "housing" is not adequate for modern standards, which could trigger administrative action such as building code inspections, child welfare investigations, or social service outreach, such as Adult Protective Services.

According to a 2021 report by The Plumbing Poverty Project, in New York City alone, 65,000 residents are estimated to not have access to piped water. As you explore the map, consider this, and what it might mean for people in New York. See the bar chart below for summary representation of this data in a bar chart. You can click on the buttons to toggle between owner-occupied and renter-occupied housing units.
